Your Drawings Are Your Exit Strategy

What AI won't tell you about escaping copycat prior art. Part of the startup patent strategy series.

By Andrew Leung, founder of W&Patent
Canonical source: https://wpatent.com/draw-first-write-second.htm

The same AI that collapsed your build time can generate a provisional from your public footprint and write your patent text and generic diagrams. AI won't draw your exit strategy for you. Every sketch is a fallback position you can retreat to when prior art shows up.

The Possession Principle

You can only claim what you possessed on filing date. The USPTO does not care how elegant your writing is. It cares whether a person of ordinary skill would agree you had the invention on day one.

Drawings are the most direct evidence of possession. A rough sketch proves you possessed that specific shape. A thousand words describing "a bracket" proves almost nothing when prior art shows up with any bracket at all.

The rule: you can always claim less than you disclose. You can never claim more than you possessed.

If you file with... You can claim... You cannot claim...
Text only — "a bracket" A bracket (broad) An L-shaped bracket with 45-degree gusset
Drawing + text of L-shaped bracket with gusset A bracket or L-shaped bracket or L-shaped with gusset Nothing — you have all three options

With text only, you have one claim position. With a drawing, you have three — and the two narrower ones are your exit routes when the broad one gets blocked.

What AI Won't Tell You

AI drafting tools generate specification text. They generate claims. Some generate the same generic diagrams from your prompt. None of them tell you: the most valuable thing in your provisional is not the text. It is the sketch you drew while thinking through the problem.

A five-minute sketch of an edge case — a variation, an alternative angle, a specific dimension — that sketch is a claim you can retreat to three years from now. An AI-generated description of the same thing is broad, generic, and gives you no narrowing path.

AI won't tell you to draw because AI does not need escape routes. AI has no patent at risk. You do.

How It Works

  1. Draw first. Sketch your invention from every angle. Sketch variations. Sketch edge cases. Every sketch is a potential fallback.
  2. Write second. Every line in your drawing gets a reference number and a sentence. The text proves you understood what you drew.
  3. Claim third. Write broadly. Then look at your drawings and ask: "If prior art forces me to narrow, what features do I have evidence for?"

USPTO practice allows you to amend claims to add a feature if that feature is supported by the original disclosure. A drawing showing that feature is disclosure. Even if your written description never mentioned the angled bracket — if it is in the drawing, you can amend to it.

Your provisional is not a term paper. It is a chest of evidence. And the most valuable evidence you can put in that chest is not your prose. It is your pictures — because pictures are the exit routes you will need when someone else's prior art closes the main road.
